Problem of Practice
When AI can produce the work, the product stops telling the story.
Generative AI has made polished academic performance dramatically more accessible — but assessment designs were built for a world where polished performance required corresponding cognitive effort. That gap is the problem this fellowship addresses.
Validity Failure 1
Illusion of Fluency
Polished outputs that do not reflect understanding. The artifact signals learning that did not occur.
Validity Failure 2
Illusion of Participation
Completed tasks that do not reflect engagement. Submission is mistaken for learning.
Validity Failure 3
Invisible Advantage
Students who use AI strategically but undisclosed gain an unearned credential signal. The assessment cannot see what it was designed to measure.
What Became Visible at CNG

Two classroom pilots — Statistical Conspiracy (Accelerated Geometry) and Systems Under Pressure (Algebra 2) — surfaced a pattern: when students made their reasoning visible, teachers learned things about student understanding that traditional assessment alone could not have revealed.

Visible reasoning protocols gave teachers access to where students changed their minds, where AI deepened understanding and where it replaced it, and which misconceptions persisted. These moments were invisible in final products but visible in the reasoning process.

The Most Important Finding

"The strongest evidence of learning often appeared not in the final product, but in the student's explanation of how and why their thinking changed."

That insight drives the design of every protocol in this fellowship.

The Organizing Question
"If AI completed this task perfectly, what learning would disappear?"
When the answer is everything — the task needs redesign.
When the answer is nothing significant — the design is working.

VSR — Used Every Cycle, After Every Redesign
2× per Year
Every assessment redesign ends with a VSR session — one for each cycle. After each enactment, the teacher reviews a short clip of their own classroom. The central question is always the same: What did I think would happen at this moment, and what actually happened?
VSR Session 1 · Cycle 1
Cajina facilitates. Uses an AI-TPACK lens to examine where AI appeared unexpectedly and what the gap reveals about the teacher's design knowledge.
VSR Session 2 · Cycle 2
Peer-facilitated within the pair. Cajina attends as a silent moderator — present but not leading. The teacher's pair partner uses the same lens. Tests whether the reflection practice has been internalised.
The Protocol
What is VISTA?
VISTA — Visibility Into Student Thinking and Agency
A framework and protocol for making student learning processes observable — examining how students reason, decide, and revise, not just what they produce at the end.
V

I

Student
T

& Agency
VISTA creates visibility into how students reason, make decisions, revise ideas, and exercise agency throughout learning — rather than focusing only on what they produce at the end. Rather than asking "is the answer correct?", VISTA asks: "how did the student arrive here, what did they change their mind about, and where is the genuine thinking in this work?" In AI-rich classrooms, where polished products can be generated without corresponding understanding, this visibility is what makes assessment meaningful.
Without VISTA
A polished essay or analysis. Teacher doesn't know: Did the student understand the topic? Did they use AI? Which parts did they actually think through?
With VISTA
The essay plus: a revision note showing where the student changed their argument, an AI disclosure log, a 90-second reasoning vlog. Teacher can see the thinking.

How This Connects to CNG
CNG PriorityConnection to This Fellowship
Vision & MissionDeveloping teachers who can design learning that remains meaningful, visible, and assessable when AI shapes how academic work is produced.
Core ValuesIntegrity in assessment (honest evidence of what students know). Responsibility for professional judgment. Caring for student agency and human-centered design.
SAIL FrameworkEvery redesigned assessment includes explicit SAIL positioning (L1–L5) with teacher justification. Teachers learn to make AI-use decisions deliberately, not by default.
Science of LearningAssessment designs are evaluated against Dehaene's four pillars (attention, active engagement, error feedback, consolidation) and Neuroteach principles. The VISTA audit asks: does this task still produce the conditions under which brains learn?
Innovation Learning InitiativeStructured professional inquiry, evidence-based iteration, and institutional knowledge building. Year 1 evidence base prepares Year 2 department-level implementation led by the AI Task Force.
