Step 2 Learning Vulnerability Classifier
What type of learning is at risk in this task? Check all that apply — this shapes your redesign pathway.
Procedural
Procedure & Fluency
Step-by-step execution, algorithmic processes, skill repetition through doing. The learning lives in the practice itself.
e.g. Solving systems of equations, titrating in a lab, conjugating verbs, geometric construction
Conceptual
Concept & Understanding
Making meaning, building mental models, understanding why something works. Requires genuine sense-making — not just recognition.
e.g. Why exponential growth outpaces linear, how natural selection works, what makes a source unreliable
Evaluative
Evaluation & Judgment
Weighing evidence, making disciplinary judgments, applying the epistemic standards of the field.
e.g. Evaluating a historical source, deciding if a statistical claim is causal, critiquing a scientific model
Creative
Creation & Expression
Original making, design decisions, expressive choices. The learning lives in the decisions made during creation — not in the product alone.
e.g. Designing a data visualization, writing with voice, composing, building a physical model
Embodied / Tacit
Embodied & Tacit Knowledge
Knowledge that lives in the body, in craft, in physical or spatial practice. Must be performed — it cannot be fully transferred through text.
e.g. Athletic technique, laboratory dexterity, spatial reasoning in geometry, pacing and breath in PE

Step 4 SAIL Positioning
Not a default setting — a deliberate pedagogical decision. Circle the level that protects the learning you identified. Be ready to justify your choice.
Select Level What AI May Do What Learning This Protects
L1
No AI		 AI is off-limits. Brain, books, academic sources only. Full cognitive load on the student. Use when the struggle itself is the learning — first drafts, initial problem-solving, forming interpretations before any external support.
L2
Ideate		 AI can help get ideas. Student thinks and writes themselves. Student does all substantive work; AI sparks or unsticks. Use when the learning is in the doing, not in knowing where to start.
L3
Edit		 AI helps improve existing student work. Student creates first. Student retains authorship; AI refines. Use when the learning is in the decisions and substance — not in polish and mechanics.
L4
Evaluate		 AI does the work. Student decides how well it answers the prompt. Evaluative judgment is the learning. Use when the student's ability to assess quality and explain criteria is the disciplinary goal.
L5
Co-Create		 AI as learning partner. Student and AI create together — student cites. Creative leadership and epistemic authority. Use only when this VISTA audit confirms the core learning cannot be bypassed.
Justify your SAIL positioning
Why this level and not one above or below? What specifically about your learning goal and vulnerability rating led you here?

Final Audit Final Audit — After Full Enactment
Complete after you have taught the redesigned assessment and collected student work. These questions are harder — they ask about what actually happened, not what you planned.
This is not the same as the Initial Audit. You should have already completed a full cycle — audit, redesign, enactment, evidence collection — before working through this.
Section A — What Actually Happened
What did the student reasoning artifacts reveal that your initial audit didn't anticipate?
Be specific. Name one thing that appeared in the student work — a misconception, a moment of genuine understanding, a pattern — that you didn't predict.
Where did AI deepen student understanding, and where did it replace it?
Don't generalize. Identify a specific student or moment where you could see which was happening.
What did the visible reasoning protocol actually make visible — and what did it still hide?
What did you learn from the reasoning artifacts that you couldn't have learned from the product alone?
Section B — Tensions and Unresolved Questions
What disciplinary tension did this cycle surface that you are still sitting with?
Not a design problem you can fix. A genuine tension between your discipline's epistemic values and what AI makes possible.
What did this cycle reveal about your own assumptions — about students, learning, or AI?
What belief did you hold going in that the evidence complicated?
What question about AI and your discipline are you now carrying that you weren't before?
This is your research contribution — not a solved problem, but an open question this cycle has sharpened.
Section C — How Your Thinking Has Changed
How has your understanding of what AI can and cannot do in your discipline changed?
Specific to your subject — not a general statement. What does AI handle well and where does it break down in ways that matter for learning?
If you were to redesign this task again for next year, what would you change and why?
Backwards design from what you actually observed. What does the evidence tell you the task should have asked for instead?
What would you say to a skeptical colleague in your department who hasn't started this work?
Not a pitch. An honest account of what you found hard, what surprised you, and what you'd want them to know first.
